What is the Student Mentorship Program?

The Student Mentorship Program (SMP) at AYBU (Ankara Yıldırım Beyazıt University) aims to provide guidance and support to students through a mentee-mentor system. Its goal is to help students adapt to campus life, understand the course structure and academic processes, develop effective study skills, encourage participation in extracurricular activities, and access the support systems they need. Effective Mentoring Skills

Effective mentoring requires more than just common sense. It involves specific, identifiable skills that contribute to learning and change. Research by Linda Phillips-Jones (1977) revealed that successful mentors and mentees use distinct processes and skills throughout their partnerships. These skills can be learned and help create more productive and enjoyable relationships. Without structure and these skills, mentoring relationships can be ineffective, leading to frustration and the belief that mentoring doesn’t work.

Core Skills for Both Mentors and Mentees

The following are key skills both mentors and mentees should utilize in their mentoring relationships:

1. Listening Actively

Active listening is the foundation of effective mentoring. It builds trust and strengthens relationships by demonstrating that you genuinely hear and understand your mentors or mentees. Key behaviors of active listening include:

  • Show genuine interest through responses like “Hmmm...” or “Interesting...” and occasionally paraphrase their words to reflect understanding.
  • Avoid interrupting while they speak, allowing them to finish their thoughts before you respond.
  • Remember past conversations and show interest in previous topics, e.g., “How did your meeting with the manager go?”
  • Summarize key points to ensure mutual understanding.
  • Use nonverbal cues such as eye contact, nodding, or leaning in to show attentiveness and empathy.

Resist the urge to immediately offer solutions or turn the conversation to your own experiences. Focus on listening and understanding first. Encourage a reflective approach where both you and your partner can explore the issue more deeply before problem-solving.

If your mentoring partner tends to solve problems quickly, guide them toward exploring the issue through listening first. This helps in developing more thoughtful solutions.

2. Building Trust

Trust is fundamental in any mentoring relationship. It develops gradually through consistent, reliable actions. To build trust, mentors and mentees should:

  • Keep confidences and respect privacy.
  • Spend quality time together to demonstrate commitment.
  • Follow through on promises to show reliability.
  • Respect personal boundaries and preferences.
  • Own up to mistakes and work on solutions.
  • Express disagreements tactfully to maintain honesty without causing harm.

In cross-difference mentoring (e.g., gender, culture, age), trust-building is especially important. Patience, respect, and openness can bridge these differences and strengthen the relationship.

3. Encouraging

According to Phillips-Jones, encouragement is one of the most valued mentoring skills. It boosts confidence and motivation. Research shows that positive feedback is rare in many organizations, but mentors and mentees alike thrive when they receive genuine praise.

Effective mentors encourage mentees, and vice versa, to keep the relationship productive and motivating. Here’s how to provide effective encouragement:

  • Compliment accomplishments and traits like perseverance and integrity.
  • Praise privately, but also consider public recognition based on cultural preferences.
  • Express thanks and appreciation regularly.
  • Write encouraging messages or voicemails.
  • Let them know how their support helps you.

Ensure that praise is sincere, and aim for more positive comments than corrective ones, ideally four or five to one.

4. Identifying Goals & Current Reality

Both mentors and mentees should have a clear sense of their personal goals and the current reality of their situations. As a mentor, understanding your mentee's aspirations helps align their goals with their reality. Mentees should also be clear about their own strengths, weaknesses, and specific needs before seeking help.

To improve this skill, mentors and mentees should:

  • Know what’s most important to them and their core values.
  • Recognize strengths and areas for improvement.
  • Set realistic short- and long-term goals.
  • Assess their current abilities and situations accurately.

Continuous self-reflection and feedback are key to fine-tuning goals. By modeling this skill, mentors can help mentees bridge the gap between their current reality and desired outcomes.

Critical Skills for Mentors

In addition to the core mentoring skills described above, mentors use several specific competencies in an attempt to help mentees develop:

1. Instructing/Developing Capabilities

Mentors often teach and guide their mentees in informal ways, helping them learn new skills, knowledge, and perspectives. This involves:

  • Acting as a “learning broker” by helping mentees access resources like books, websites, and people.
  • Teaching new skills and ideas through explanations, examples, and thought-provoking questions.
  • Helping mentees understand their organization’s culture, history, and values.
  • Modeling effective behaviors and guiding mentees to monitor their progress.
  • Teaching the mentoring process by making mentees aware of mentoring activities and their purpose.

Effective instructing involves more than just sharing knowledge—it’s about facilitating learning and growth through practical guidance and feedback.

2. Inspiring

Great mentors inspire their mentees to pursue their own greatness by setting an example and exposing them to other inspirational people and experiences. You can inspire by:

  • Leading by example and challenging mentees to improve.
  • Helping mentees observe other inspiring figures and experiences.
  • Encouraging them to strive for meaningful goals and actions.
  • Helping mentees recognize their own past inspiring actions.

Rather than imposing your own goals on mentees, inspire them to discover and pursue their own version of greatness.

3. Providing Corrective Feedback

Effective mentors provide corrective feedback when mentees make mistakes. This should be done with care to ensure the mentee is receptive. Key steps include:

  • Using positive, professional language when addressing mistakes.
  • Giving feedback in private, soon after the event.
  • Offering specific, actionable suggestions for improvement.
  • Ensuring the feedback aligns with the mentee's growth goals.

Make sure your mentee is open to corrective feedback, and always balance it with positive reinforcement.

4. Managing Risks

Effective mentors help mentees navigate risks and avoid major mistakes. This skill involves:

  • Helping mentees recognize and assess risks in their actions.
  • Providing advice to avoid major business or career mistakes.
  • Encouraging mentees to prepare well and trust their decisions.
  • Acting as an advocate when needed, especially in difficult situations.

Mentors should offer strategies for managing risks, helping mentees take calculated risks while protecting them from unnecessary mistakes.

5. Opening Doors

Mentors can provide visibility and opportunities for their mentees by connecting them with the right people. This includes:

  • Making introductions to influential contacts.
  • Highlighting mentees’ abilities to others in their organization.
  • Giving mentees opportunities to work with important colleagues and customers.
  • Suggesting additional resources or opportunities to pursue.

Opening doors for mentees should be done only when they are ready, ensuring they are capable and trustworthy before presenting opportunities.

Critical Skills for Mentees

In addition to the core skills described earlier, mentees need to be competent in several areas:

1. Acquiring Mentors

Becoming a successful mentee requires active engagement in finding and negotiating with mentors. You should:

  • Identify potential mentors who align with your goals and needs.
  • Actively seek out and approach multiple mentors.
  • Convince potential mentors to guide you, explaining your needs and goals.
  • Clearly communicate your goals and needs to mentors.
  • Negotiate mentoring arrangements, including expectations, confidentiality, and meeting schedules.

Being proactive and clear about your needs is essential to finding the right mentoring support.

2. Learning Quickly

Mentors appreciate mentees who are quick to learn. As a mentee, you should strive to:

  • Apply new knowledge and skills and inform mentors about how you’ve used them.
  • Observe mentors and others to learn indirectly.
  • Study relevant materials to enhance your development.
  • Integrate new knowledge into your problem-solving framework.
  • Receive feedback openly and non-defensively, seeking specifics and being appreciative.

Being an active learner, open to new ideas and feedback, will help you progress faster in your development.

3. Showing Initiative

Effective mentees take the initiative to manage their relationships. You should:

  • Know when to show initiative and when to follow your mentor’s lead.
  • Ask clarifying questions to deepen your understanding.
  • Take the initiative to pursue useful resources independently.
  • Take informed risks to acquire new knowledge, skills, and experiences.
  • Go beyond your mentor’s suggestions and apply creative approaches.

Balance initiative with understanding your mentor’s preferences to ensure a productive relationship.

4. Following Through

Following through on commitments is vital in a mentoring relationship. To demonstrate this skill:

  • Honor all agreements made with your mentors.
  • Complete tasks on time and report back the results.
  • Explain if you need to change or break an agreement in advance.
  • Persevere with difficult tasks even when discouraged.

Consistently following through shows that you are dedicated to your own development and respectful of your mentor’s time.

5. Managing the Relationship

You should take responsibility for managing your mentoring relationships by:

  • Understanding and explaining the mentoring process.
  • Staying up-to-date with each mentor on progress, goals, and meeting schedules.
  • Assessing the current status of mentoring relationships and deciding next steps.
  • Ending formal mentoring relationships amicably, with the possibility of continuing informally.

Regularly evaluating the mentoring relationship and making adjustments as needed ensures continued growth and mutual satisfaction.
